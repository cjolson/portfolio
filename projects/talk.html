Client

 

Talk: The New Sex Ed

 

A Pittsburgh based non-profit dedicated to providing teens and their parents with essential sex education.

 
 
 

CHALLENGE

 

How do we reach more people?

 

Talk wants to reach more people, but is limited in its current classroom format. How can it leverage a digital solution to provide its education to more students?

 
 
 

Solution

 

Sex Education through Interactive Text-Based Narrative

 

We proposed a story-based model with embedded sex ed content that students found engaging, accessible, and informative.

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

Features

 
 
 

Interactive Narratives

 
 

Embedding our educational content in text-based narratives was a relatable way to engage today’s teens. Our stories read like “hot gossip”, but connected with students on a level they were familiar with and didn’t feel overtly educational.

 

We introduced decision points for users to make choices on behalf of the characters in the story. This prompted students to think critically about the content and affirmed how their decisions had consequences without labeling any decision as “right” or “wrong”.

 

Some decision points were designed to test student knowledge and trigger them to question or doubt how much they know.

 
 
 

Educational Resources

 
 

In embedding educational content in a narrative story, we learned that objective and factual information still needed to be provided by an authoritative source for our students to trust it. We introduced resources shared in the context of the story for content such as this.

 

Students could choose to click into these resources if they desired, or continue with the story uninterrupted. Resources provided in the story were also available after they completed the narrative so the information was not lost.

 
 
 

Scalable Framework

 
 

Talk required a solution they could grow independently. We envisioned an interconnected web of stories that our client could add content to as resources become available by introducing new concepts and characters to the existing platform.

 

Being a non-profit, it was important to provide them freedom and flexibility in the development process. We wanted our solution to be achievable and easy to build upon as Talk gets bigger.

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

Process

 
 

Generative Research

 
 
 

In the early stages of our process, we focused on understanding and defining the scope of our client’s problem.

 

To approach this we built out a comprehensive stakeholder map of those involved in the sex education process. This became a tool for us to frequently refer to when considering who is involved in this process and our relationship to them.

 

We also spent time researching the current state of sex ed by doing a competitive analysis and literature review.

 
 
 

One iteration of our Stakeholder Map. It was updated as we learned more.

 
 
 

Literature Review, Competitive Analysis, Stakeholder Mapping, Concept Mapping

 
 
 
 
 

Seeking to understand the broad experience of sex education programs, we interviewed students, parents, teachers, and health professionals. We also observed Talk in the classroom firsthand. We used data from these observations and interviews to build an affinity diagram that helped us identify relevant themes.

 

These findings were used to map the student journey, identify design opportunities, and identify the strengths of our client’s that set their program apart from other sex ed curriculum.

 
 
 

The team building an affinity diagram from our interview notes.

 
 
 

Interviewing, Affinity Diagram, Journey Mapping 

 
 
 
 

Need Validation

 
 
 

Some of the themes we identified in our generative research informed needs we wanted to validate. We illustrated different ways to meet these needs in different contexts and shared these with our target users.

 

The feedback we received helped us understand our users’ boundaries and identify promising directions we could pursue in ideation.

 
 
 

Two of many storyboards used to need validate.

 
 
 

Speed-Dating

 
 
 

Ideation

 
 
 

At this point we had many ideas. As a team we brainstormed a broad number of concepts and quickly prototyped low fidelity samples of the ideas we wanted to understand better. We invited our target users to use the prototypes and provide feedback.

 

We were able to quickly narrow down a direction based on what users found engaging and educational.

 

This entire process helped us define our design goals based on insights we identified from talking to our users.

 
 
 

Some early low fidelity prototypes.

 
 
 

Rapid Prototyping, Think Aloud Protocol

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

Design Goals

 
 
 
 

Engaging and Relevant

 
 

Insight

 

Teens think they know a lot of things they don’t or believe that a lot of sex education content isn’t relevant to them personally.

 
 
 

Goal

 

Teens think they know a lot of things they don’t or believe that a lot of sex education content isn’t relevant to them personally.

 
 
 
 
 

Embedded Design

 
 

Insight

 

Teens expressed strong disinterest in anything they viewed as extracurricular education.

 
 
 

Goal

 

We had to prioritize presenting educational information in an entertaining context that was not perceived as explicitly educational.

 
 
 
 
 

Empowering Teens

 
 

Insight

 

Students appreciate feeling independent and value experiences that provide a sense of autonomy.

 
 
 

Goal

 

We must nurture a learning experience that empowers teens and allows them to explore their values without feeling judged.

 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 

Iterative Design

 
 
 
 

From our rapid prototyping feedback, our chat fiction interactive stories stood out as a framework that met our goals.

 

We proceeded with a process of iterative design where we experimented with and refined interactions, user flows, narrative script, and visual elements to ensure the whole experience was engaging and desirable. We continued to host research sessions with our users to receive feedback on these designs.

 

Through this process we were able to make informed decisions that led us to our key features.

 
 
 

We iterated on our prototype in features, content, and aesthetics.

Reflections

 
 

We Captured the Essence of Talk

 

We unknowingly recruited a student of our client in the late stages of design. Although we did not disclose our connection to the client, she commented how similar she felt our prototype was to her experience at Talk.

 

Satisfied Client

 

Our client was incredibly satisfied and excited about our proposed solution. Talk’s next steps include developing the chat fiction interface and writing stories that will teach the first wave of educational content.

 

Other Areas to Explore

 

Some other directions to explore in this space include tools to help facilitate parent/teen communication regarding sex ed and and service that prompts teens to reflect on their own life and define their values.
